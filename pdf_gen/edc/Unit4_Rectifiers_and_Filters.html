Unit 4: Rectifiers & Filters (EDC)

Q.1 What is Half Wave Rectifier? Explain performance parameters of HWR. 6 Marks
Definition (1 Mark)

A Half-Wave Rectifier (HWR) is an electronic circuit that converts only one half-cycle of an Alternating Current (AC) input into Direct Current (DC) output.

Performance Parameters (5 Marks)
  • 1. Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV): The maximum reverse voltage a diode can withstand without breaking down.
    PIV = \( V_m \)
  • 2. Rectification Efficiency (\(\eta\)): The ratio of output DC power to input AC power. It tells us how well AC is converted to DC.
    \(\eta = \left(\frac{P_{out(DC)}}{P_{in(AC)}}\right) \times 100 = 40.6\%\)
  • 3. Ripple Factor (\(\gamma\)): A measure of the purity of the DC output. Lower value means better quality DC.
    \(\gamma = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_{rms}}{V_{dc}}\right)^2 - 1} = 1.21 \text{ (or } 121\% \text{)}\)
  • 4. Transformer Utilization Factor (TUF): The ratio of DC power delivered to the load to the AC rating of the transformer secondary.
    TUF = 0.287
Q.2 Explain performance parameters of FWR. 4 Marks
Performance Parameters of Full-Wave Rectifier (FWR)

A Full-Wave Rectifier converts both half-cycles of the AC input into DC output, making it more efficient than HWR.

  • 1. Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV): Maximum reverse voltage across the non-conducting diode.
    PIV = 2\( V_m \) (for Center-Tap)
  • 2. Rectification Efficiency (\(\eta\)): It is double that of a Half-Wave Rectifier.
    \(\eta = \left(\frac{P_{out(DC)}}{P_{in(AC)}}\right) \times 100 = 81.2\%\)
  • 3. Ripple Factor (\(\gamma\)): It is much lower than HWR, meaning the output is smoother.
    \(\gamma = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_{rms}}{V_{dc}}\right)^2 - 1} = 0.48 \text{ (or } 48\% \text{)}\)
  • 4. Transformer Utilization Factor (TUF): Better utilization of the transformer compared to HWR.
    TUF = 0.693 (for Center-Tap FWR)

Q.4 What is a filter? Explain the need & types of filters. 4 Marks
1. What is a Filter? (1 Mark)
  • A filter is an electronic circuit placed after a rectifier.
  • Its job is to remove the unwanted AC components (ripples) from the pulsating DC output and allow only pure DC to reach the load.
2. Need of Filter (2 Marks)
  • The output from a rectifier is not pure DC; it is pulsating DC (contains both AC and DC).
  • These AC ripples can cause noise, instability, and damage to sensitive electronic devices (like radios, TVs, computers).
  • Therefore, a filter is needed to smooth out the waveform and provide a stable, constant DC voltage.
3. Types of Filters (1 Mark)
  • 1. Capacitor Filter (Shunt Capacitor)
  • 2. Inductor Filter (Series Inductor)
  • 3. LC Filter (Choke Input Filter)
  • 4. CLC Filter (\(\pi\)-Type Filter)
Q.5 Explain Capacitor Filter in detail. 6 Marks
Connection & Principle (2 Marks)
  • Connection: A large capacitor (C) is connected in parallel (shunt) with the load resistor (\(R_L\)).
  • Principle: A capacitor offers low reactance to AC (passes AC to ground) and infinite reactance to DC (blocks DC). Thus, it bypasses the AC ripple and lets the DC pass through the load. It acts as a storage tank, charging during peak voltage and discharging when voltage drops.
Working / Operation (4 Marks)
  • Charging Phase: When the rectifier output voltage is increasing, the diode is forward biased. The capacitor quickly charges up to the peak voltage (\(V_m\)).
  • Discharging Phase: When the rectifier output voltage starts to drop below the capacitor's voltage, the diode becomes reverse biased (turns OFF). The capacitor then slowly discharges its stored energy through the load resistor (\(R_L\)), maintaining the voltage across the load.
  • Re-charging: In the next cycle, as soon as the input voltage exceeds the capacitor voltage, the diode turns ON again, and the capacitor quickly recharges to \(V_m\).
  • Result: This continuous charge-discharge cycle prevents the load voltage from dropping to zero, creating a much smoother DC waveform with a small ripple.
Q.6 Explain Inductor Filter in detail. 6 Marks
Connection & Principle (2 Marks)
  • Connection: An inductor (choke coil 'L') is connected in series between the rectifier and the load resistor (\(R_L\)).
  • Principle: An inductor opposes any change in current. It offers high reactance to AC (blocks ripples) and zero reactance to DC (allows DC to pass).
Working / Operation (4 Marks)
  • When the pulsating DC from the rectifier passes through the inductor, the inductor opposes the rising current by storing energy in the form of a magnetic field.
  • When the rectifier current starts falling, the inductor opposes the falling current by releasing its stored magnetic energy back into the circuit.
  • Result: This opposition to both rising and falling current smooths out the sudden variations (ripples) in the load current.
  • Key Property: Higher the value of inductance (L), the smoother the current. Inductor filters work best for heavy loads (low \(R_L\), high current).
Q.7 Explain LC Filter in detail. 6 Marks
Construction & Principle (2 Marks)
  • Construction: It is a combination of a Series Inductor (L) and a Shunt Capacitor (C). It is also called an "L-Section" or "Choke Input" filter.
  • Principle: It combines the advantages of both components. The series Inductor blocks the AC ripple, and the parallel Capacitor short-circuits any remaining AC ripple to ground. Pure DC flows to the load.
Role of Bleeder Resistor (\(R_b\)) (4 Marks)
  • A bleeder resistor (\(R_b\)) is connected in parallel with the capacitor and the load.
  • Why is it needed? An inductor requires a continuous flow of current to function properly. If the load is removed or is very light (high resistance), the current through the inductor might become discontinuous.
  • If current is suddenly interrupted, the inductor produces a massive back EMF (\( V = L \cdot \frac{di}{dt} \)).
  • This high voltage spike can destroy the rectifier diodes (exceeding PIV) and puncture the filter capacitor.
  • Conclusion: The bleeder resistor ensures a minimum, continuous current always flows through the inductor, preventing dangerous voltage spikes, thus protecting the circuit.
Q.8 Explain CLC (\(\pi\)) Filter in detail. 6 Marks
Construction & Principle (2 Marks)
  • Construction: It consists of a shunt capacitor (\(C_1\)), a series inductor (\(L\)), and another shunt capacitor (\(C_2\)). Because its circuit diagram looks like the Greek letter Pi (\(\pi\)), it is called a \(\pi\)-filter.
  • Principle: It acts as a two-stage filter, combining a capacitor input filter (\(C_1\)) with an LC filter (\(L\) and \(C_2\)).
Working / Operation (4 Marks)
  • Action of \(C_1\): The first capacitor \(C_1\) acts just like a normal shunt capacitor filter. It charges to peak voltage and removes a majority of the AC ripple. However, the output is still somewhat pulsating.
  • Action of L: The series inductor L offers high impedance to the remaining AC ripple and drops the AC voltage, blocking it from passing to the load.
  • Action of \(C_2\): The second capacitor \(C_2\) bypasses any tiny remaining AC ripple that made it past the inductor, safely sending it to ground.
  • Advantages: Because it uses three filtering elements, the ripple factor is extremely low. It provides a very smooth, almost perfect DC output voltage. It is widely used in power supplies.
